UX Research

UX research is described as doing thorough research into the needs of users and incorporating the findings into the UX design process. To analyse user behaviour and uncover issues that influence the entire UX, the study involves a variety of methods and methodologies. UX research encompasses a variety of investigative methods used to add context and insight to the design process. Unlike other sub-fields of UX, research did not develop out of some other field or fields. It is merely translated from other forms of research. In other words, UX practitioners have borrowed many techniques from academics, scientists, market researchers, and others. However, there are still types of research that are fairly unique to the UX world.



UX research methodologies may also be divided into two categories:


Quantitative Research

Any study that can be quantified is considered quantitative research. It provides answers to inquiries like “how many people clicked here” and “what proportion of users can find the call to action?” It’s useful for estimating statistical probabilities and figuring out why a website or app isn’t working.

Qualitative Research

Soft research is a term used to describe qualitative research. It provides solutions to queries such as “why didn’t individuals see the call to action” and “what else did they observe on the page?” Interviews or chats are frequently used to do this. Qualitative research aids us in comprehending why individuals behave in the way they do.

Interaction Design

The practice of developing interactive digital goods and services is referred to as interaction design or IxD. Interaction designers pay attention to how people engage with goods and utilise communication principles to create the experiences they want. The phrases “interaction design” and “user experience design” are sometimes used interchangeably. This is logical given how closely interaction design and user experience design overlap. After all, UX design is all about enhancing the user’s experience with a product, and interaction between the user and the product is a big part of that. However, user experience and interaction design are not the same things. The way we approach user interactions is the key difference between UX and interaction design.
